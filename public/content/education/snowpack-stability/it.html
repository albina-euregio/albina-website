Stabilità del manto nevoso

La stabilità del manto nevoso, insieme alla distribuzione della frequenza della stabilità del manto nevoso e alla dimensione delle valangheè uno dei fattori chiave nella valutazione del grado di pericolo valanghe utilizzando la Matrice EAWS. In seguito all'Assemblea generale dell'EAWS tenutasi a Davos nel giugno 2022, tutte le tre variabili sono state definite e corroborate da esempi utilizzati nella pratica. Definizioni, commenti, esempi e informazioni più approfondite per la variabile stabilità del manto nevoso si trovano nelle sezioni seguenti o su avalanches.org.

Definizione

Snowpack stability is a local property of the snowpack describing the propensity of a snow-covered slope to avalanche (Reuter and Schweizer, 2018). Snowpack stability is described using four classes: Very Poor, Poor, Fair, and Good.

Commenti

A seconda del tipo di valanga, la stabilità del manto nevoso è descritta da: 

  • Failure initiation, crack propagation and slab tensile support (snow-slab avalanche) (Reuter and Schweizer, 2018)
  • Loss of strength/bonding (loose-snow avalanche) (e.g. McClung and Schaerer, 2006)
  • Loss of basal friction and slab tensile and/or compressive support (glide-snow avalanche) (e.g. Bartelt et al., 2012)

Snowpack stability is inversely related to the probability of avalanche release. Snowpack stability describes the snowpack to fail given a specific trigger (Statham et al., 2018), as for instance a person skiing a slope. The term local refers to a point which ranges in size from a potential trigger location or stability test to a starting zone.

All snowpack stability assessments may refer to either future (forecast) or present (nowcast) based on observations or models. For instance, if the snowpack in a release area is considered Fair today, and until tomorrow a layer of new snow is expected, the stability of tomorrows snowpack including the new snow layer needs to be assessed. Likely, it has changed to Poor or even Very Poor by that time.

Most prominent examples are spontaneous avalanches, signs of instability or classical snowpack stability tests, such as the Extended Column Test (ECT) or the Rutschblock Test (RB). Arrows indicate that existence towards lower stability classes is imperative. Natural avalanches are a clear indication for the class Very Poor, while a low and a high additional load are considered approximately equivalent to Poor and Fair snowpack stability. Observations and stability test results should be regarded as indicative only.

For wet-snow stability and glide-snow stability, the separation between the classes Fair and Poor is often difficult. Wet-snow avalanches most often release naturally and are therefore connected to the class Very Poor. Observations will often only provide a tendency towards either Good or Very Poor stability (Figures 2 and 3). Stability tests generally work poorly in wet snow. If they indicate Very Poor stability, they should still be considered, but not otherwise.
