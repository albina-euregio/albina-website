Snowpack stability, along with the frequency distribution of snowpack stability and avalanche size, is one of the key contributors when assessing the avalanche danger level using the EAWS Matrix. Since the EAWS General Assembly in Davos in June 2022, all three variables were defined and underpinned with examples used in practice. Definitions, remarks, examples and more in-depth information for the variable snowpack stability can be found in the following sections or on avalanches.org

Definition

Snowpack stability is a local property of the snowpack describing the propensity of a snow-covered slope to avalanche (Reuter and Schweizer, 2018). Snowpack stability is described using four classes: Very Poor, Poor, Fair, and Good.

Remarks

Depending on the avalanche type, snowpack stability is described by:

  • Failure initiation, crack propagation and slab tensile support (snow-slab avalanche) (Reuter and Schweizer, 2018)
  • Loss of strength/bonding (loose-snow avalanche) (e.g. McClung and Schaerer, 2006)
  • Loss of basal friction and slab tensile and/or compressive support (glide-snow avalanche) (e.g. Bartelt et al., 2012)

Snowpack stability is inversely related to the probability of avalanche release. Snowpack stability describes the snowpack to fail given a specific trigger (Statham et al., 2018), as for instance a person skiing a slope. Table 1 shows the relation between snowpack stability and how easy it is to trigger an avalanche.

Table 1: Stability classes, and the type of triggering typically associated with these classes.

Stability class How easy is it to trigger an avalanche?
Very Poor Naturals / very easy to trigger
Poor Easy to trigger (e.g. a single skier)
Fair Difficult to trigger (e.g. explosives)
Bona Stable conditions

The term local refers to a point which ranges in size from a potential trigger location or stability test to a starting zone.

All snowpack stability assessments may refer to either future (forecast) or present (nowcast) based on observations or models. For instance, if the snowpack in a release area is considered Fair today, and until tomorrow a layer of new snow is expected, the stability of tomorrows snowpack including the new snow layer needs to be assessed. Likely, it has changed to Poor or even Very Poor by that time.

Relation between the snowpack stability classes and typical phenomena or observations

Figure 1 shows examples for common evidence or indications for snowpack stability classes focusing on dry-snow slab avalanches release. Most prominent examples are spontaneous avalanches, signs of instability or classical snowpack stability tests, such as the Extended Column Test (ECT) or the Rutschblock Test (RB). Arrows indicate that existence towards lower stability classes is imperative. Natural avalanches are a clear indication for the class Very Poor, while a low and a high additional load are considered approximately equivalent to Poor and Fair snowpack stability. Observations and stability test results should be regarded as indicative only.

For wet-snow stability and glide-snow stability, the separation between the classes Fair and Poor is often difficult. Wet-snow avalanches most often release naturally and are therefore connected to the class Very Poor. Observations will often only provide a tendency towards either Good or Very Poor stability (Figures 2 and 3). Stability tests generally work poorly in wet snow. If they indicate Very Poor stability, they should still be considered, but not otherwise.
