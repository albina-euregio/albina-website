Following a severe avalanche accident in Roger's Pass, Glaciers National Park, BC (Canada) in February 2003, Parks Canada and Avalanche Canada created and developed the Avalanche Terrain Exposure Scale (ATES).The ATES is a cartography that aims to help all users of the winter mountain to assess the severity of the avalanche terrain in the planning phase of the outing. The three types of terrain - Simple, Demanding and Complex - describe the degree of exposure to avalanches and their consequences.

ATES zoning is obtained from the analysis of a set of terrain parameters at a given resolution that fails to assess the characteristics of the terrain at a slope scale.For this reason, ATES is not a navigation tool during departure.

Simple terrain

Exposure to uneven slopes and forest land.Some clearings in the forest may involve infrequent avalanche arrival areas.Lots of options to reduce or eliminate exposure.

Demanding terrain

Exposure to well-defined avalanche areas, exit areas or traps.There are options to reduce or eliminate exposure by finding routes carefully.

Complex terrain

Exposure to multiple and overlapping avalanche areas or large tracts of open and sloping terrain. Multiple avalanche start areas with low traps.Minimal options to reduce exposure.

Transitions

The boundaries between the different terrain categories are not an exact line, but there are transition strips.This is reflected in the maps as an area where the colors overlap in a width of 20 m.

Trajectory of usual avalanches

Areas where natural avalanches occur relatively frequently, especially in situations of recent snow, melting due to rain, or melting due to high temperatures.
